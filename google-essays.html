1. What developed your interest in computer science? How will the EP program support your future goals?

Growing up in the Valley, I always heard about the tremendous potential of CS, but didn’t fully realize its impact until my 
junior year internship at Stanford, where I researched methods of predicting survivorship for brain-tumor patients.

Before interning, CS was syntax on a screen that maybe compiled and ran. However, my work continued, I realized 
that my code was playing quasi-God, predicting the chance that a real person, with a family and emotions, would live 
or die, based on the code’s identification of trouble-some oncogenes. If the code failed, life would be lost. That 
realization was powerful: code wasn’t just words anymore, but rather, a living extension of myself and humanity.  

My dream is to start a bioinformatics company that efficiently processes anatomical data into meaningful results: like 
taking the speed of blood flow and predicting clot patterns. Googlers often deal with enormous heaps of data, and as 
an EP applicant, I hope to work alongside them, learning about the fastest, most accurate methods of data processing, 
so that I too can realize my dream of touching and saving precious lives.   

CS isn’t syntax: it’s me. It’s my way to leave the world better off than I entered it.

2. Tell us about a time you’ve used your strongest coding language. Please go into detail about your experience using
this technical language (for example project, competition, website).

As a rising senior, I worked at Stanford’s Department of Radiology on the Brain-Atlas project, whose premise involved
predicting survival rates for brain-tumor patients based on differential epigenetic expression and tumor location. I was
tasked with helping to write the atlas’s back-end, in PHP supported by JAVA and R.

Over the course of four months, I learned how to read text and CSV survivorship values using the read.table() functions 
in R, and hold these values in vector and matrix structures, each with a set of attributes like gene name and location. For 
example, to quickly search for a gene, I implemented a version of binary search to sort and traverse the R vector, isolating 
the gene in question and printing out its unique attributes.

To interface R with JAVA and create a display “applet”, I initially used Java/R Interface, an API package with displayPlot() 
and outputPlot() functions to display survival curves for visitors by providing R vectors as arguments. However, I slowly 
transitioned to using ShinyPlot, an R graphing API, to plot the survival curves and histograms, using JAVA and ShinyPlot 
to provide frames for data plots and binary search to traverse the R vectors for user-selected data in ShinyPlot.

3. The EP program is committed to increasing diversity within the technology industry. Why do you feel having a diverse
workforce is important and what can Google do to further this goal in technology?

“I feel trapped at hackathons. The guys are elitist and make me feel stupid.”
“Am I an engineer? I’m not Asian.”

I’ve heard those statements too many times to turn a blind eye. Diversity powers innovation, especially in engineering where
engendered stereotypes about non-traditional engineers have stalled creativity. Companies actively cut their talent pool by
ignoring workforce diversity, ignoring brilliant ideas coming from non-traditional engineers that have unimaginable potential.
Disregarding diversity perpetuates antiquity, failing to identify novel perspectives.

Google plays a pivotal role in increasing workforce diversity, given its importance in industry. To push an agenda of diversity,
Google needs to continue placing non-traditional engineers in impactful positions. Gayathri Rajan and David Drummond are
excellent examples of what Google needs to continue: giving young girls and minorities role models to emulate throughout
their lives and careers. Google also needs to start younger: send non-traditional engineers - women and minorities - to give
presentations at elementary schools and provide scholarships to children interested in code: preempt the stereotype that
engineering is for certain people.

The fight for diversity isn’t just about workforce diversity: it’s about engineering equality. Google and other companies can’t let stereotypes decimate talent, a fight that is desperately worth fighting.
